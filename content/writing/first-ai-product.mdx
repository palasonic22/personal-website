I spent six months building an AI-powered workflow tool for a specific niche. Here's what actually mattered.

## The model is not the product

Everyone talks about which model to use. GPT-4 vs Claude vs Gemini. In practice, the model is the easiest part. The hard part is figuring out what problem you're actually solving and how to make the AI feel invisible.

The best AI products are the ones where the user doesn't think about the AI at all. They just think: "this tool understands what I need."

## Speed beats polish

I shipped v1 in three weeks. It was rough. The prompt engineering was amateur. The UI was ugly. But I learned more from those three weeks than I would have from three months of planning.

The best feedback came from people using it in the wild, not from internal testing. When you watch someone struggle with your onboarding in real time, you learn things no survey will ever tell you.

## The prompt is the product

This is the thing that surprised me most. The quality of your prompts determines 80% of the user experience. Not the model choice, not the infrastructure, not the UI. The prompts.

I spent more time iterating on prompts than on any other part of the product. And that's where the real differentiation lives. Anyone can wire up an API call. Very few people can write prompts that feel like they understand the user.

## What went wrong

- **Over-engineering the tech stack.** I spent a week choosing between different frameworks when I should have just picked one and built. The user doesn't care about your architecture.
- **Not building a feedback loop early enough.** I should have had a "was this helpful?" button on day one. Instead I waited until v3 and had to rebuild the data pipeline from scratch.
- **Trying to be too general.** The first version tried to serve three different use cases. Each one was mediocre. When I narrowed to one, the product became good.

## What I'd do differently

- Spend more time on the onboarding experience. If users don't understand what the tool does in the first 30 seconds, they'll never come back.
- Build a feedback loop into the product from day one. Not as an afterthought. As the core of the development cycle.
- Stop overthinking the tech stack and just ship. The best framework is the one you can build in.

## The broader lesson

AI changes the economics of building. Things that took a team of five can now be done by one person. But the bar for what constitutes a good product hasn't lowered — it's actually higher. Because now there are a thousand people building the same thing.

The differentiation isn't the technology. It's the taste. It's the ability to know what to build, who to build it for, and when to stop building.

That's the part that's hard. And that's the part that's worth getting good at.

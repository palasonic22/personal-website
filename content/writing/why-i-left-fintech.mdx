After spending years in fintech and crypto, working on payments infrastructure that handled millions in volume, I started asking myself a simple question: was I building things I actually cared about?

The answer was increasingly no.

## The scale trap

There's a seduction in scale. When you work on products that touch millions of users, every decision feels weighty. You optimize for the 99.9th percentile. You run A/B tests on button colors. You write post-mortems for outages that lasted three minutes.

None of this is wrong. But it narrows your world. You stop thinking about whether a product should exist and start thinking about how to move a metric from 3.2% to 3.4%.

The problem isn't the metrics. The problem is that metrics become the ceiling of your ambition. When your entire job is to optimize a number, you forget that the number was supposed to represent something real — people using your product, solving a problem, getting value.

## The comfort of complexity

There's also a kind of status attached to working on hard problems. "We process 10 billion transactions a day" is a sentence that sounds impressive at dinner parties. It's harder to explain why a WhatsApp bot that helps you split dinner bills is meaningful, even if it's the thing you actually enjoy building.

But complexity is not the same as value. I've seen teams spend six months building a feature that moved a metric by 0.1%. I've also seen a single weekend project solve a problem for hundreds of people in a way that mattered to them.

The hard work is knowing the difference.

## What changed

I started building small things on the side. A WhatsApp bot for tracking expenses. A simple landing page for a friend's project. A Notion template for planning product launches.

These shipped in days, not quarters. People actually used them. I learned more from launching a weekend project than from shipping features at a company of 500 people.

The feedback loop was immediate. I'd put something out, someone would use it, they'd tell me what worked and what didn't, and I'd fix it the same day. At scale, that cycle takes months. By the time the data comes back, the team has already moved on to the next sprint.

## The freedom of small

There's a specific kind of freedom in being small. You don't need to write a design doc. You don't need to get alignment from five stakeholders. You don't need to defend your choices in a meeting with people who haven't spoken to a user in months.

You just build.

> The best products I've worked on were built by people who cared more about the problem than the process.

That's not an argument against process. Process exists to prevent disasters. But process also prevents magic. And if you're too big, you'll optimize for disaster-avoidance over magic-creation every time.

## What now

I'm building small products independently. Writing about what I learn. Exploring AI workflow automation for Singapore SMEs.

The scale is smaller. The learning is faster. And I'm having more fun.

I don't know if this is permanent. I don't know if I'll go back to building at scale. But I know this: the next time I join a large company, I'll bring this perspective with me. The instinct to ship small, to test fast, to care about the thing and not just the metric.

And if I don't go back — that's fine too. There are worse ways to spend your time than building things people actually use.
